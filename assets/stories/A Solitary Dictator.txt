It took me a full twenty-six days to reach the other side of the continent, and here, everything seems to have stood still: the vegetation is sparse, and I have come across very few animals along the way. The grass is low, the trees give the impression of being evergreen, and I have the sense of travelling through a wholly uninhabited land, at the mercy of the biting cold I feel despite the season. I would like to describe the journey at greater length, to tell you how these lands have astonished me, but in truth they are rather… monotonous. A little green, endless steppe, mountains in the distance: beautiful oceans of greenish fields which, after a few days, began to wear me down. I cannot fathom how my forebears could have inhabited such inhospitable lands, but I suppose they must have had their reasons for doing something so utterly foolish… after all, they are the same people who decided to bury much of the planet in concrete. At least, however, I have reason to believe I saw something that has been missing from the mountains of my homeland for many centuries: it was white and far away, only on the highest peaks, but I think Mater used to call it "snow". In my youth I loved hearing about snow — that mystical substance that falls from the sky and, like a veil, whitens everything beneath it. Mater would also invent absurd stories — of how the ancients used to throw it at one another, and how they used it to make strange little artefacts… it sounds far too entertaining to be true.

This long road has brought me to the threshold of my next destination: Xùtnij is a city so imposing that it is considered a nation in its own right. Now that I see it from a distance, it has the same colour as snow, but appears far more luminous… it rises like a spectre over the valley, with its tall, vertical white walls giving the impression of a jagged, inhospitable coastline. My client had already warned me to fly a red and blue flag on my cart, so that no one would attempt to attack me before my arrival, and that alone was enough to make me feel unsafe throughout this pilgrimage. Around Xùtnij lie the ruins of what I would have to call the corpse of a long war: dwellings perforated with bullets, craters left by who knows what ordnance, the odd bridge shattered by the madness of conflict and by the force of time. Travelling through an abandoned city that lies at the foot of a fortress only deepened my growing unease: all I can smell is death, and not the kind that brings nostalgia. The steppe has by now been erased by chaotic rubble, collapsed apartment blocks and yawning chasms in the asphalt. I press on with my macabre march through the void, hoping to glimpse some human face come to greet me, but… not a soul utters a sound.
The feeling of solitude grows in step with my progress; though reaching this place has been the longest journey I have ever undertaken, this agony seems infinitesimally interminable. I still see, among the walls, fragments of civilisation and of daily life: a kitchen waiting only to be of use to someone, shoes that will never run again, a doll that wants only to forget. The pain I can sense from this place is so great that I find myself wondering only what could have unleashed it all. I have studied wars, conflicts, and the times when human beings could no longer be kind, but to find oneself face to face with history is something else entirely.

I reach the gate of Xùtnij only towards evening, hoping never to wander through this stone ghost again. I had hoped for some comfort, but standing before the city-fortress only begins to make me grasp the source of so much pain: from a distance the walls are white, but the sealed entrance gate is covered in a stain of dull ruby — likely shifted by years upon years of attempts to breach it. The enormous gateway rises twenty metres high, almost reaching the scale of the entrance to Sonos, but here it is plain that the architect was not an uncontrollable wind, but mere human caprice. There are no kind ladies or stooped old men awaiting me: my welcome consists of two vast firearms — turrets that follow my every movement as if I were a fly under a chameleon's eye. They are very rare these days; no one feels the need to threaten their fellows anymore. Yet these seem preserved like favoured children, raised and tended as if they were meant to provide for their master's old age. A metallic voice booms out a short distance away: "At last he deigns to present himself! I have been awaiting your visit for far too long! Have you perhaps lost your way? Did you not know where the resplendent capital lay? I doubt it — everyone knows my Xùtnij. Come now, do not concern yourself with my little beasts and step forward — the conveyor will bring you to me!" No greeting, no real questions, only imperatives and commands. I advance with my cart; the platform on which I stand is hooked by something and begins, slowly, to crawl. The gates, forgotten by time but not by rage, begin to part — for me alone.

What I see inside the fortress astonishes and confuses me at once: some manner of magnetic system drags my cart along a predetermined route, presenting me with a panorama of curious and original structures, all of them built in ivory-coloured brick. These skyscrapers seem a jumble of styles: some buildings form an arch, others are grafted one atop the other, one even coils upon its own crown. The longer I look at the structures within the walls, the more they seem to have been added piecemeal, as if the need to make new places for new people had arisen all at once. The trouble is that… the people themselves do not exist. I see no citizens: what looks like a communal market is utterly deserted; not a person at the windows, not so much as a pious soul walking the pavements. I can describe what I am beholding only in spectral terms; the capital is a wholly white skeleton, while all its flesh has long since rotted away. The conveyor halts only after two long hours, at the centre of this tangle of immaculate disquiet, before the largest structure in this knot of sorrow. After a few minutes the door swings open, and a small, stout little man emerges. "YOU! WELCOME TO MY BELOVED Xùtnij! AT LAST YOU HAVE HAD THE GOOD FORTUNE OF LAYING EYES UPON THE CAPITAL THAT EXISTS THANKS TO MY KINDNESS ALONE!" — as short as he is… loud. The man before me is my new client, Tuskin, who, on my comlog, styled himself the "supreme absolute emperor" of the capital. I am here to meet someone I viscerally despise; I had only ever heard of him through the porters, and I would never have wished to work for a worm of his sort… but I am also convinced that history must be told, lest it be repeated.
Tuskin escorts me into his palace, his villa, his personal castle: there are so many riches and works of art in its halls that all the museums of the world combined could not contain them. "Do not let my little jewels intimidate you! They are but a small portion of what I possess! My wealth is for the people, not for me, of course. You see, here in the palace everything is automated: guards, security, gardens, cooks — I do not lift a finger and have need of no one! Xùtnij was the capital of technology; we automated everything, even the management of unrest. For a few decades after the fall I heard a fair bit of clamour outside, but my systems are impregnable." There — now everything is explained. Tuskin lives outside reality, in a bubble of cognitive seclusion… and his technology must have killed and driven away his own people.

"I have invited you here for a very specific purpose: my collection is so imposing that it includes pieces the world itself has by now forgotten. I want you to make a portrait of my magnificent person before the last painting of the famous painter of Xùtnij — surely you know his name? The one of the murals, I mean." No — I explain that, regrettably, I have no notion of whom we are speaking, but his attention is already elsewhere: Tuskin is distracted by a squeak in the distance. "Hm, again. You should know that I have lately been troubled by rats — I am convinced they are skulking through the electrical systems of the security plant. For years now I have heard these pests moving inside the walls." I nod, feigning understanding: I have lived in communion with nature since I was a child, and these do not sound like rats at all. After several walks we finally reach the hall in which the famous painting is displayed: a masterful canvas, ten metres by five, depicting the fall of some city I do not recognise — perhaps even the one that lies beyond the walls. I see the pain the artist felt in every brushstroke, the rage he was feel— "So, anyway, this is the thing — quite a beauty, as you see; I'm convinced my voters will adore a portrait of me with this rubbish in the background" —ing… I have no words. I try to change the subject before the rite, and before fleeing, asking WHICH voters. "What do you mean, which? The ones who are HERE! They are everywhere in the city! Do you not see how many people are walking the streets? How many workers crossing the squares? You passed two schools to reach me — don't tell me you didn't see the students!" He speaks of all this as though it were happening here and now, but… there is no one in Xùtnij. I try to tell Tuskin that I have not seen a living so— "IMPOSSIBLE! Are you, perhaps, calling me a liar? I HAVE NEVER LEFT MY PALACE, BUT MY CAMERAS SEE THEM! THEY HAVE ALWAYS BEEN THERE!" —ul. It is unclear to me what is happening; I am certain I saw no one in the city, but I have no wish to throw further fuel onto this fire.

I ask Tuskin to pose; I am ready to be rid of this dreadful place. The dictator places himself at the centre of the enormous painting, tries a pair of poses and expressions, and at last settles on one. "Good! I am ready — when you are, proceed!" I lift the relic, but feel it heavy, almost as if it had become a boulder… and at the same moment I begin to hear the sound from before. At first only a few squeaks, then a hundred, a thousand, and at last millions of them. They arrive all at once, from every direction, almost as if they had been waiting for the rite to begin. "ENOUGH, NOW! SYSTEM! ELIMINATE THE THREATS!" "-*no threats detected*-" "SYSTEM! DO NOT CALL ME A LIAR! FIND THE RA—" I carry on with my work, capturing Tuskin in the moment of absolute panic, almost as though my instinct, once again, had taken over. When the sound subsides I hand him his copy, but his face turns white, drawing dangerously close to the colour of his city. "What… what are you handing me?" I cast a glance at what I have given him… Tuskin is well-centred as he shouts and writhes, calling for his security system. The trouble lies in the surroundings: all around him are a multitude of people, hundreds of thousands hurling themselves from the walls and massing about Tuskin, their faces blazing with a primordial ra— "YOU! HOW DO YOU KNOW THEM? WHERE DID YOU MEET THE VOTERS? THIS IS A WICKED JOKE!" —ge. What I see is the past — a past of carnage and innocents, a past that even Tuskin recognises, for he is now weeping like a child.

I am far from Xùtnij by now. I do not know whether anything in the heart of a centuries-old dictator has changed, but those tears will remain kept within my mind: I had hoped they would have been less bitter.
