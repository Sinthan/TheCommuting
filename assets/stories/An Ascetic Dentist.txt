It had never happened to me before — receiving a request from a dentist. You hardly find them anymore these days, and if it weren't for my curiosity to meet one, I doubt I would ever have braved the pilgrimage through the desert. The journey to reach the city felt endless, and the worst stretch was the Unifier: standing against a wind that strong without being swept away proved a far harder challenge than I had anticipated. The region has been abandoned for years now, populated by nothing but sand and debris, yet the capital Sonos endures as the last bulwark of the civilisation that once inhabited these lands. The Unifier, with its monstrous force, dragged the surrounding buildings inward and gathered them into a single central zone, shaping the concrete like clay. What had once been a forest of skyscrapers has become one vast and unbroken structure, and the populations began migrating into that enormous architectural skeleton because everything else was eroded by the wind, the lands had grown unworkable, and any complex smaller than Sonos was either swept away or absorbed into the city itself. I have often wondered whether the people of these lands ever considered turning nomadic, riding the moods of the Unifier in tiny, hyper-resilient houses — but perhaps I speak as an ignorant outsider, and perhaps nothing can survive the force of that demon.

Sonos, on the other hand, lived up to every expectation I had of it: terrifying and admirable at once. I had imagined it enormous and statuesque, but only once I stood at the foot of its walls did I understand how it had endured: the skyscrapers had melded into a single megalopolis, shaped by the wind and bowed under their own immense weight. The city reminds me of a tortoise's back, curved and stretched eastward, and the windows of what were once palaces are now elongated, like vast wrinkles cut into the shell of this weary creature. There are no visible seams between one building and the next; everything seems incredibly amalgamated, chewed, almost like flesh spat out after being gnawed to the marrow. The paint, of course, did not survive, and the absence of colour only deepens the impact of those bare and grey remains… If I am not mistaken, the ancients had a word for such peculiar styles — I believe it was "gothic". They were certainly fond of giving names to the things they made, but Sonos was not made by human hands. Entering the city is almost as overwhelming as admiring it from outside: the immense archway, hewn into the concrete by the inhabitants themselves, serves as the main and only means of entering and leaving the city. A vast and crude ceiling looms above me, forming an enormous, endless dome that becomes my new sky. I see the figure of the dentist waving to me, Lisa Albeniz, identical to the photograph I received on my Holosign. She tells me she insisted on collecting me at the entrance herself, because although Sonos seems a single entity from outside, within it is a labyrinth of walls, houses and structures piled and fused upon one another. They appear to work on the archway at least once a week: were they to stop excavating, the Unifier might erode and reshape it, sealing the entrance shut again.

Lisa is strikingly kind. She leads me to her quarters, which we reach only after a winding, labyrinthine route. I remember passing through a square, then a kitchen, a bedroom, and what looked like a bathroom. "Personal space no longer exists in Sonos," Lisa tells me. "By now everyone shares everything; we use privacy only as currency. You want five minutes alone to change? Then try selling yourself as a barista to make someone a coffee. You need an hour to say goodbye to a dying relative? You'll have to work a full day. The entrance hasn't been kept open for our sake, to allow us to leave — no, people don't even think of leaving anymore. They're too busy selling themselves." The notion is foreign to me, and I am not used to so many people pressed together… let alone passing through their rooms as if it meant nothing. Since I began travelling I have lived in complete solitude: being a pilgrim makes any lasting company impossible. Lisa's rooms are the only ones that seem to retain a measure of intimacy: there are curtains, walls, and above all, not a soul in sight. "I'm extraordinarily busy," Lisa goes on. "There are no real doctors left in Sonos, and I'm the only one who can, in some way, help the sick. I hold many credits in the city, which is why you see so few people around here: a single visit of mine is worth at least five hours of privacy. Lately the work has been consuming far too much of my time, which is why I requested your services. I needed to share all of this with someone."

Lisa begins moving things around, clearing space inside her medical room — the largest she has. "Of course, saying 'has' is practically a joke around here, but I imagine it's easier for someone like you to follow. Maybe you enjoy sharing things, but in your existence it remains a choice. In this city we cannot afford to decide whether or not to share something. Individuality would be our death." The reality of this god-forsaken outpost strikes me like a cold shower. Lisa is moving a bloodstained cot, a small table covered in scalpels, and a chair I assume she uses for examinations — but none of it is hers by right. Sharing is imposed; the objects belong to Sonos, and it is Sonos that allows her to keep them, because she herself is something Sonos owns. We manage to clear enough space to sit on the floor; it's time I began doing what I am here for. I set down my heavy pack and take out my tools. "I think it's the first time I've seen one," Lisa remarks. "I know they used to be in everyone's pocket, long ago, but I suppose by now they're considered obsolete." With a faintly bitter smile, I find myself explaining once again the past of an object that has by now become a companion to me: yes, the world was once full of these little devices, and every human being knew how to use them. Today, because of technological advances and neural implants, nobody would have the slightest idea how to make them work, let alone how to maintain them. "God, I'd love to travel the world too, to be among people who need a little company. I always thought that if I hadn't become a dentist I would have followed some elder to learn the language of base-reality… but leaving Sonos is only a fantasy for those born here." Her words carry a heavy sourness. "The work is exhausting, and lately the Eye epidemic has been spreading without rest. I removed a molar from a man only yesterday. I thought it was a simple cavity, but once I'd extracted the tooth I looked more closely. Four eyeballs, in the usual black miasma…" I pull a face, hoping she'll understand I have never heard of anything of the sort. "Ah, right — sorry. The Eyes don't appear outside Sonos. Every so often a miasma manifests here on random places, organic or inorganic. It comes like a faint dark mist on pipes, on people, on walls, on anything. And in the mist the Eyes wallow: they are eyeballs in every sense, tiny or enormous, in any dimension, colour and shape you can imagine. They submerge, they peer, they observe, but they don't seem to come from any actual body or to be connected to any kind of nerve. I tried to extract them with my tools, but they simply evaporate." The thought sends a cold shiver down my spine. Eyes? I have heard of strange diseases, but never of organs that grow in the mist… Lisa pulls the molar from her pocket, and I begin to focus — we are now at the heart of the ceremony. "I hate this place. I would have liked to see the world and to escape this prison." I nod, and carry out my movements. After a few seconds I hand her the relic, the result of the rite. She studies it, and I can almost hear her implants complaining about all that ancient mechanism. "And yet… well… here, people rely on me. We don't exist outside our city, and I would be nothing without these instruments or my patients. Keep your copy carefully — one day it might prove useful to those who have never seen the Eyes. Perhaps they'll find a cure." I understand her feeling, but only in part — as I do the city. I am an ignorant outsider; I can intuit, but I have not lived through what they have lived through.

I take leave of Lisa as I move away from Sonos, leaving that tomb behind, accompanied by a faintly bitter aftertaste on my palate. At least I managed to dilute her feelings a little, and now it is time to continue with my routine: I leave it all behind me, and turn my cart toward the next destination.
