I was in the city, taking a walk to get some fresh air, just to keep myself from staying indoors again. It felt strange — quiet — yet the jog posters were still out, distributing flyers to the citizens to advertise the new circus that was due to arrive that very day. The air was crisp, the day cloudy and faintly melancholy, but on the whole pleasant enough. I was on the main avenue when I began to notice the first wagons at the far end of the street. I deduced that the circus was already arriving, since I could see a sizeable group of people walking toward me, presumably following what I imagined to be a show of colour, light and sound. The music coming from the distance was somewhat dismal, but I have always been the one with a problem regarding the circus: it has traumatised me ever since I was a child — though this time, the feeling seemed to be mutual.

The people heading toward me were not following the joyous procession; they were fleeing it. I could see them running like madmen, putting as much distance between themselves and the commotion as they could. I thought of an accident, of some act of terrorism, but the music kept on and the people kept running. Seized by fear, I hid myself in one of the new cardboard bins they had installed weeks earlier; I did not know whether to let myself be swept up by the crowd or to follow it, and shutting myself in an iron drum seemed the more intelligent solution. Through the bin's slats I could still see outside, but the first thing to strike my senses was the pungent smell of a butcher's shop. I thought some idiot had thrown organic refuse into the wrong bin, but the smell was, indistinguishably, coming from outside. I decided to peek, to look through the slats without making myself noticed; perhaps I might catch some clue as to this strange foulness. Never had I imagined I might see something of this kind in the world of the living, with these very eyes.

The sight that presented itself was a parade. The circus was, in fact, arriving in town — but the better question was: where the hell was that circus coming from? The first to come close enough to my field of vision were jugglers mounted on draught animals: they stared straight ahead, eyes wide and lost in the void of the buildings; they smiled as though seized by a facial paralysis, while spinning brick-red spheres. What I could not begin to explain, however, were the draught animals themselves — those beasts resembling hideous slugs. They were the size of a horse, viscid and creeping, the red of muscle, covered in pulsing veins. They moved sluggishly along the asphalt, drawing the first wagon and leaving a trail of what was, to all intents and purposes, blood.

Behind them I saw the wagon itself, large as a three-storey house, creaking heavily in time with the circus's dismal music; I could almost see it sway, as though it were a precarious structure, red and black. Walking alongside the wagon passed what I can only describe as an infernal monkey — extremely tall, almost three metres; I watched it walking beside the wagon, unchained and free to move as it pleased, yet remaining faithful to that slow advance. From my hole I managed to glimpse three of them, shrivelled and possessed of impossibly long limbs. Their nails scratched the asphalt, and on their faces — a horrible grin, framed by mad yellow eyes — overrode all reason.

I wake every night with the nightmare of that parade; I still see those horrible creatures sneering in the dark… but I have not yet described the worst of it. At the parade's end, drawn by a few men, came a solitary wagon — enormous in comparison to the others — and it contained something extremely small. There was a great deal of empty space around the object; it did not seem usable space, but rather a silent respect that had been left there. I saw something black, something black of a kind I had never seen before — darker than night itself. I could not bear it; I fainted as that wagon went by, and woke only hours later.

The circus is in town now, motionless in the central square for weeks — immobile, oblivious, intangible. No one visits it, no one enters it: we are all too afraid. We dread what horrible spectacles might be presented beneath that blood-red tent… and people keep disappearing without trace, the population keeps dwindling, beneath that dreadful little tune.
