There are legends that speak of a particular wind — a wind that is never catalogued, being far too anomalous. You know — there exist currents such as the Sirocco, the cold breaths of the Mistral, or the sandy whistles of the Khamsin; but the wind of Dunchersnatch is indecipherable, mysterious, wholly random and obscure to most of the inland peoples. It is said to come from the north, from regions unknown to human eyes, but its origins have never been proven. One legend tells that the Dunchersnatch comes from a frozen forest: an ancient green region struck by a sudden glacial curse. According to the tales, the trees of Dunchersnatch are still intact beneath their thick veils of ice; they no longer sprout, but they refuse to die or to obey the natural laws — and it seems that the smallest displacement of air can give rise to a portion of this wind that perpetuates itself in the atmosphere, travelling across the globe.

The wind of Dunchersnatch manifests without warning. Thus, while you are enjoying your walk, you may run into a sudden, freezing breeze — not unpleasant in itself, light and merely very cold… but for what this current carries with it. The Inuit speak of the Dunchersnatch as a wind to be kept far from; the most ancient clans of these peoples have lived in close contact with it, and tell of this wind as an omen of death, as the very howl of the departed. At its arrival a chill is felt deep within, in the very soul; it always unleashes a heavy weeping and a sense of solitude. One is, in short, overcome by a sensation of perdition, as though our spirit were already convinced of being destined for the other world. But the worst of it is what you might hear inside the current itself.

According to the tales of the elders of the most ancient Inuit clans, the wind emits a most peculiar and unique sound: they believe that the whisper of the Dunchersnatch is the whisper of the souls of the dead. Indeed, at its arrival, one can hear an unceasing weeping and lament — cries of pain, calls for help. It is said that these voices come from the frozen forest, that they reverberate through the iced wood. The Inuit hold that the forest is the mouth of hell, that the cries of the dead are trapped in the passage of the Dunchersnatch, and that one must never listen to its whispers. The whispers of the Dunchersnatch are the curses of the fallen; to listen to them would only lead us to know secrets we did not seek, to hear of things unnameable, to perceive the will of those who have been close to us.

So, the next time you wander carefree with your friends, with the love of your life, pay close attention to the sound around you — to the sudden breezes that make your skin crawl. The wind of Dunchersnatch strikes without mercy. It might bring you counsel, but the voice of the dead ought to remain silent… Remember: those who listen to the words of the departed end up like them — or, even worse, alive, but conscious of what could befall them, awaiting their own turn to end up in the Dunchersnatch.
