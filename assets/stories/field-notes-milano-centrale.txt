Field Notes: Milano Centrale

The station was built to make you feel small. This was intentional. Mussolini commissioned it and the architects obliged, giving him vaults so high that clouds could, theoretically, form inside the hall if conditions were right.

They are never right. But you think about it anyway.

I arrived on a regional train that smelled of coffee and warm plastic and the particular anxiety of commuters who have been sitting too long. The platform was fourteen. I always count platforms; it is a habit from childhood when I believed that numbers held information, that if you paid close enough attention to them the world would become legible.

It does not. But you keep counting.

Milano Centrale is a building that takes itself seriously in a way that is almost endearing. Outside, the fascist marble reliefs show workers and horses and the heroic postures of people going somewhere with great conviction. Inside, the gelato costs four euros and the Wi-Fi requires registration.

I found a bench near the centre of the main hall and sat for an hour. This is something I recommend: sit in a large train station with no purpose except to be present. Watch people arrive and depart. Notice how they move — the weight of luggage changing posture, the way a person searching for a platform walks differently from a person who knows exactly where they are going.

An old woman in a blue coat moved through the crowd like she had been moving through crowds her entire life and was tired of it and was going to continue anyway. A group of students argued in a language I didn't catch, their voices bouncing off the marble.

The departure board clicked and reshuffled. Somewhere in its mechanism, decisions were being made, translated into light and letters, broadcast to hundreds of people who read them and then moved.

I did not read the board. I had nowhere to be for another two hours.

This is the luxury of deliberate waiting: the world performs for you without requiring participation.

A pigeon landed near my feet, considered me, decided I was neither useful nor dangerous, and left.

I wrote this down.
