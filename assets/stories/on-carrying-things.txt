On Carrying Things

My bag has a broken zipper on the left pocket. I have known about it for two years. I have not fixed it because fixing it would mean acknowledging that I intend to keep the bag, and I am not ready to commit.

This is how I feel about most things.

There is a theory that you can understand a person entirely by what they carry. Not what they own — ownership is just accumulation, a slow geology of want and habit — but what they choose to have on their body when they move through the world. The contents of a bag in transit are a kind of manifesto.

Mine contains: a notebook I don't write in often enough, two pens (one out of ink, kept for reasons I have not examined), a camera that is too large for casual carrying and which I carry anyway, a paperback novel whose bookmark has not moved in three weeks, charging cables for devices I only sometimes have with me, a bag of emergency almonds, and something I found on the ground in Lisbon and have not yet identified.

The almonds are important. I forget to eat when I travel and then become a person I don't recognise — impatient with lines, unable to make decisions, susceptible to purchasing things in airports.

The camera is the thing I think about most. It is too heavy. It marks me as someone with intentions. When you carry a large camera, strangers look at you and wonder what you are documenting, and sometimes they become self-conscious, and sometimes they become generous — performing their own lives a little more deliberately, holding their coffee cup at a better angle, sitting up straighter.

I don't always photograph these moments. Sometimes I just watch. The camera makes watching permissible; it gives the looking a purpose, even when there is no photograph at the end of it.

I think a lot of things work this way. The notebook gives the noticing permission. The paperback novel provides an alibi for sitting alone in public. The broken zipper defers the question of whether any of this is permanent.

In Kyoto, a woman on the train asked me what I was writing. I said I wasn't sure yet. She nodded as if this was a reasonable answer, because it was, and we rode in silence for two more stations, and then she got off and I watched her walk down the platform with the specific purposefulness of someone who knows exactly where they are going, and I thought: I would like to feel that, sometime. That clean directedness. That unburdened stride.

Then the doors closed and the train moved on and I was still there, with my bag and my broken zipper and my unread novel and my almonds, going somewhere I had chosen more or less at random, for reasons that were improving the longer I was in motion.
