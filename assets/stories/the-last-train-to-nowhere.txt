The Last Train to Nowhere

There is a particular quality of light that only exists in train stations after midnight. It falls flat and yellowish on the marble floors, indifferent to the few travellers scattered like forgotten luggage across the benches.

I had a ticket to somewhere I no longer remember. This happens, sometimes — you buy a ticket as an act of faith, believing that by the time the departure board flips to your platform, you will have remembered why you bought it.

The man beside me was reading a newspaper folded so many times it had become a kind of origami grief. He turned the pages without reading them. I understood this. There are gestures we make not because they produce anything but because the alternative — sitting perfectly still, doing nothing — feels like admitting something we are not ready to admit.

A child ran through the hall, her shoes squeaking against the floor. She stopped, looked up at the enormous vault of the ceiling, and for a moment the whole station was in her expression: the weight of the arches, the indifference of departure times, the small private happiness of velocity.

Her mother called. She ran on.

I checked my ticket again. The train was delayed. Of course it was. Trains to nowhere are always delayed — they have nowhere specific to be, and this freedom is also a burden. They leave when they leave. They arrive when they arrive. In the meantime you sit, and the light falls flat, and the man beside you turns pages without reading them, and somewhere a child is discovering that large spaces are not empty, only waiting.

The board flipped. My platform number appeared.

I picked up my bag. I did not remember why I was going. I went anyway.

This is, in the end, what travel is: the willingness to be in motion before you have understood the destination. The destination is almost never the point. The point is the posture — the body angled toward something, the eyes open, the ticket still warm from your hand.

I got on the train.

The lights of the station receded.

Outside, there was only dark and the occasional smear of a town seen from the inside of speed. I pressed my forehead to the glass and watched it all go past, unresolved and luminous, and I thought: this is enough. This has always been enough.
